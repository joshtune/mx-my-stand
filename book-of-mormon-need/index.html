Why do you need the Book of Mormon if you have the Bible?

Because God promised more witnesses, and He kept the promise — the Book of Mormon stands alongside the Bible as another testament of Jesus Christ, not in place of it.

scripturebook-of-mormon Updated May 3, 2026

Why this matters

This is often the first question a Christian friend asks me, and the real concern under it is rarely hostile — it’s “are you adding to the Bible?” That’s a serious worry, and Revelation 22:18-19 is usually where it lands. Anyone who loves the Bible should ask this question. What I want to do here is show why receiving the Book of Mormon is not a move against the Bible, but a move with the pattern the Bible itself sets.

Where I stand

The Book of Mormon does not replace the Bible; it joins it. Two records of God’s dealings with His children — one through the line that became the Old and New Testaments, one through a branch of Israel led to the Americas — both witnessing of the same Christ. Holding both is more faithful to what Scripture claims about itself than holding either alone.

The reasoning

  1. God’s pattern is multiple witnesses. “In the mouth of two or three witnesses shall every word be established” (Deut 19:15; 2 Cor 13:1; Matt 18:16). This isn’t a marginal verse — it’s a judicial principle God repeats across both Testaments. A second witness to Christ is consistent with how God speaks, not a departure from it.
  2. God spoke to peoples beyond the lands the Bible records. Jesus said, “Other sheep I have, which are not of this fold: them also I must bring, and they shall hear my voice” (John 10:16). The Bible itself is upfront that its record is not exhaustive of God’s dealings with humanity (cf. John 21:25). Believing God spoke elsewhere isn’t an addition to the Bible’s claims — it’s taking the Bible’s own claims seriously.
  3. Scripture explicitly anticipates more scripture. Ezekiel sees two records — “the stick of Judah” and “the stick of Joseph” — joined into one in the Lord’s hand (Ezek 37:15-19). Whatever else that passage means, it does not depict God’s word as a closed single-record file. It depicts a joining.
  4. “Adding to the Bible” is the wrong frame for Revelation 22:18-19. That warning is given inside the book of Revelation about the book of Revelation; the same kind of warning appears in Deuteronomy 4:2, long before most of the Bible was even written. If Deut 4:2 closed the canon, the New Testament couldn’t exist. The verse forbids tampering with God’s word — not God speaking again.
  5. The Book of Mormon’s purpose is the Bible’s purpose. Its title page says it is written “to the convincing of the Jew and Gentile that JESUS is the CHRIST.” That is not a competing message. That is the same message, amplified by a second voice. Read it that way and it doesn’t compete with the Bible — it agrees with it under oath.
